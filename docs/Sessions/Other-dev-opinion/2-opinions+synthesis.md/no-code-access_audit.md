Here's where I think you're underweighting things, in rough order of how much they'd change the system.
The N=5 problem dwarfs everything else you're working on. Your headline result is 5 annual Sharpe observations spanning 2021–2025, and that window is uniquely contaminated: COVID liquidity, ZIRP unwind, Mag-7 concentration, and a single CPI-driven bear in 2022. The 95% CI on a mean Sharpe of 0.915 from N=5 with that variance is roughly [-0.5, 2.3]. PSR and DSR adjust for sample size in the right direction but they can't manufacture statistical power. Before you spend another week on hedge sleeves or symbolic regression, extend the backtest to CRSP/Compustat 1962–2025 for the factor edges (value, accruals, momentum all have data going back that far). The rotational mean-reversion and volume edges may not, but the four factor edges absolutely do, and that's where your strongest claims live. Your "regime-conditional" finding is plausible but it's currently being inferred from one bear and one chop year. That's not a finding, that's an anecdote pair.
Your six surviving edges are probably two-to-three independent edges in costume. Earnings yield + book-to-market = the Fama-French value factor twice. Sloan accruals + asset growth = the quality/investment factor twice. The volume anomaly and gap-fill mean-reversion are likely correlated with each other (both are short-horizon liquidity-provision premiums). Run a PCA or rolling correlation matrix on the edge return streams. I'd bet >70% of your variance loads on 2–3 components. The implication: your "diversification across 6 edges" framing is overstating robustness. It also explains the regime-conditionality cleanly — value+quality is the longest-running known regime-conditional factor stack in the literature.
The "defensive layer" framing might be the wrong frame. You're treating the bear/chop weakness as a gap to patch with hedges. But long-puts have a structural negative expected return (the variance risk premium is short, not long, over multi-decade samples), vol-targeting on a strategy with this much regime exposure tends to compound drawdowns rather than smooth them (it sells low and buys high relative to your own equity curve), and drawdown-conditional gross reduction is just stop-loss with extra steps — known to hurt long-run CAGR in factor strategies. The honest version of "defensive layer" is a separate, uncorrelated strategy with its own positive expected return. The single highest-Sharpe diversifier to long-equity factor stacks over a 100-year sample is trend-following on diversified futures (managed futures / time-series momentum on a basket of bonds, currencies, commodities, equity indices). It's specifically positive in the regimes your stack fails — sustained directional moves, including bears. AQR, Hurst/Ooi, and Moskowitz all have public papers on this. This is bigger than every hedging primitive you listed combined and you didn't mention it once. Cross-asset isn't a "complexity vs alpha" tradeoff for trend; it's nearly free diversification with positive carry.
Your discovery engine producing zero promoted edges in N weeks is the real systemic finding, not a footnote. A search system that never finds anything is searching the wrong space, has gates that are too tight, or is finding things and your gates are correctly killing them. All three answers have different fixes. My guess: you're searching feature-space (more signals on the same universe with the same horizon and same construction), which is the most-arbed surface in retail quant. The unexplored spaces are strategy-template space (different holding periods, different rebalance triggers, conditional execution rules), universe-construction space (different filters, different industry exposures, different liquidity tiers), and risk-parameterization space (the same signal at different vol targets, kelly fractions, decay rates produces statistically different strategies). Bayesian optimization over feature-space won't fix this; you need to widen the search before deepening it. Symbolic regression and PySR are sexy but premature — they need a search space that's already producing live signal.
HMM regime detection wasn't just coincident, it was probably structurally unfixable at retail scale. VIX term structure is also reactive — VIX itself is a 30-day implied forecast that mostly tracks realized vol with a small premium. Better leading indicators exist (HY credit spreads OAS widening before equity tops, 2s10s inversion as an 12–18 month recession lead, copper-gold ratio, defensive-vs-cyclical relative strength) but the empirical lead times are noisy and getting shorter as more capital chases them. The right design move is probably stop trying to predict regimes and design strategies that don't need to. A trend-following sleeve is regime-adaptive by construction — it doesn't need a classifier. That's a structural advantage your current architecture is engineering around.
The Moonshot Sleeve as currently described is small/mid-cap factor exposure dressed in moonshot language. Long-momentum on R2K + 52-week breakout + insider clusters + earnings beat-and-raise — all four are well-documented factors that are heavily traded. The premiums exist but they're not asymmetric in the sense you want. True retail-tractable asymmetric upside lives in:

Long-dated OTM options on idiosyncratic catalyst names (you mentioned LEAPS, push this further — 18-month 25-delta calls on names with binary catalysts)
Special situations (spinoffs, post-bankruptcy equity, busted convertibles, complex sum-of-parts) — Greenblatt's You Can Be a Stock Market Genius is the canonical retail playbook, still works
Concentrated exposure to genuine paradigm shifts where you can defend a thesis (your existing PLTR/RKLB/photonics work suggests you already do this discretionarily — formalize it)

The Sortino+skewness+upside-capture objective is right; the inputs feeding into it are conventional.
Tax drag finding has a design implication you didn't draw out. -0.577 Sharpe in taxable means the core strategy is structurally not viable outside tax-advantaged accounts. For most $10K–$100K retail capital this is fine because most of it is in IRAs/401(k)s anyway, but you should make it an explicit design constraint rather than a backtest footnote. It also kills any future "managed retail strategy" framing without major reformulation.
What a real shop has that you don't:

True multi-decade backtests with delisted-name awareness and point-in-time fundamentals (you have the universe right now, you need the depth)
Bootstrap distributions of all your headline metrics, not point estimates — block bootstrap with autocorrelation preservation gives you a real CI on Sharpe, max DD, etc.
White's Reality Check or Hansen's SPA test for multiple-testing across the full search space, not just FDR within a known set
Stress-conditional backtests against named historical events (1987, 2000, 2008, 2020-Mar, 2022) as separate line items, not blended into annual Sharpes
A second human reading the work. Solo quant work is solipsistic in a way that no harness fixes. One adversarial outside reader on a quarterly cadence is worth more than the next three items in your queue.

Two smaller things worth flagging:
The deterministic measurement harness is excellent engineering but it tests reproducibility, not robustness. Bitwise-identical results across reps tell you nothing about whether a different random seed, a different start date, or a different liquidity threshold would change the conclusion. Pair the harness with bootstrap-resampled-pipeline runs and you'll catch a different class of error.
The decision diary auto-emits JSONL on every backtest, which is data collection. Where's the closing-of-the-loop — quarterly review cadence, structured post-mortem, decision-quality scoring vs outcome? Without that, you're building a beautiful audit trail nobody reads.
Direct answers to your six explicit questions:

Defensive primitives: trend-following on diversified futures, full stop. Long puts and vol-targeting are second-tier and have known costs you'll discover the hard way.
Leading indicators: HY credit spreads, yield curve, cross-asset divergences (copper-gold, defensives-vs-cyclicals). But the deeper answer is don't try to predict regimes — design around them.
Most tractable retail flow signal: post-earnings announcement drift conditional on pre-earnings options positioning. Combines free public data (earnings calendar) with OPRA (free via Schwab once you ship). 0DTE dealer gamma is real but you're trading against the people who built that data.
Discovery method ordering: widen the search space before deepening the optimizer. Bayesian opt on feature-space is solving the wrong problem.
Moonshot framing: special situations + long-dated OTM options on defensible theses, not small-cap factor exposure.
Multi-asset value: yes for trend-following on futures (high). No for "add bonds and crypto" naively (low to negative after costs).
