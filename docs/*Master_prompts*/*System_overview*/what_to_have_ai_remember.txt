✅ 1. Trend-Following Edges

These are among the most consistently profitable across markets and decades.

A. Simple Moving Average (SMA) Trend Filters
	•	Price above 200-day SMA → bullish bias
	•	Price below 200-day SMA → bearish bias

Edge: Markets trend more often than people expect, producing positive expectancy when you trade in the direction of long-term momentum.

B. Pullbacks in Strong Trends

One of the strongest repeatable edges:
	•	Identify trend using 20/50/200 SMAs
	•	Buy pullbacks to the 20–50 SMA in an uptrend
	•	Sell rallies to the 20–50 SMA in a downtrend

Works because institutions scale into positions this way.

C. Breakout Momentum

Breakouts from:
	•	Multi-week highs/lows
	•	Consolidation boxes
	•	Triangles / flags / pennants

Momentum tends to persist longer than retail expects because large players spread orders over time.

⸻

✅ 2. Mean Reversion Edges

Work best in stock indices and high-liquidity assets.

A. RSI(2) or RSI(3) Mean Reversion

Famous short-term edge:
	•	Buy when RSI(2) < 5
	•	Sell when RSI(2) > 90

Edge: Indices (SPY, QQQ) tend to revert quickly due to constant inflows.

B. VWAP Deviations

Especially intraday:
	•	Price stretched too far above/below VWAP often snaps back
Used by institutional desks for execution → creates predictable behavior.

C. Bollinger Band Reversions
	•	Close outside lower band → short-term bounce probability increases
	•	Close outside upper band → short-term drop probability increases

Edge because volatility expands then contracts in cycles.

At its core, it’s meant to simulate the workflow of a professional quantitative research desk—capable of generating trading edges, testing them historically with high fidelity, analyzing performance across time and market conditions, and then seamlessly transitioning those same strategies into live or paper trading environments using Alpaca’s APIs. The ultimate vision is a self-evolving trading engine that not only trades but also learns from its performance, refines its models, and provides transparent insights into its own decision-making process. The system is composed of several layers that mirror a modular, research-to-execution pipeline: the engine layer (various engines for alpha generation, signal combination, risk, and portfolio governance), the backtester (to simulate and validate performance and produce trade logs and equity snapshots), the analytics layer (which interprets historical and real-time performance data, calculates advanced metrics, and adjusts strategy weights through the governor), and the cockpit dashboard (a live interactive interface for visualizing everything—performance curves, PnL attribution, drawdowns, edges, risk metrics, and market context). The Trading Machine is not just meant to test strategies, but to function as a continuously learning ecosystem: each run contributes new data to the “research memory” via edge feedback loops, where the system re-evaluates and recalibrates the weights of different strategies (edges) based on historical success, volatility, and correlation.
