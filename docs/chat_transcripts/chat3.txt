when does money in trades actually start effecting what the market will do? like buying or selling 1 share obviously won’t but when will it? is there a way this can be calculated? 
That’s an excellent question — and a very deep one. Let’s break it down step by step:

⸻

💡 The Basic Idea: Market Impact

When you buy or sell a share, you’re placing demand or supply into the market. The market moves when aggregate demand and supply become unbalanced.

Your trade only moves the price if it’s large enough to:
	1.	Consume liquidity at the current best price (e.g., buy all the shares offered at $100), or
	2.	Signal information that causes other traders to adjust their orders.

So, a single share won’t move anything — but enough volume relative to the market’s available liquidity will.

⸻

⚖️ When It Starts to Matter: Liquidity vs. Trade Size

A trade’s market impact depends on:
	•	Trade size (Q) – how many shares or contracts you’re trading
	•	Daily volume (V) – how many are typically traded per day
	•	Bid–ask spread and order book depth – how much liquidity is available near the current price

A common rule of thumb used by quant traders:

Once your trade size exceeds 0.1–1% of the average daily volume, you start to noticeably affect the market.

For example:
	•	A stock trades 1,000,000 shares per day.
	•	A 1,000-share order (0.1%) might have negligible effect.
	•	A 10,000-share order (1%) could cause some slippage (the price moves against you while filling).
	•	A 100,000-share order (10%) definitely moves the market unless split up.

⸻

🧮 Quantitative Models for Market Impact

There’s no exact formula, but traders use empirical models like the Almgren–Chriss model and variants of Kyle’s Lambda:
	1.	Kyle’s Model (1985):
\Delta P = \lambda Q
	•	\Delta P: expected price change
	•	Q: signed trade size (positive for buy, negative for sell)
	•	\lambda: market impact coefficient (depends on liquidity)
Higher liquidity (deep markets) → smaller \lambda.
Lower liquidity (thin markets) → larger \lambda.
	2.	Empirical Square-Root Law (used by institutions):
\text{Impact} \approx c \cdot \sigma \cdot \sqrt{\frac{Q}{V}}
	•	\sigma: daily volatility
	•	V: daily traded volume
	•	c: constant (~0.5–1 depending on market)
This shows impact scales sublinearly — doubling trade size doesn’t double price impact.

⸻

📊 Practical Example

Suppose a stock:
	•	Trades 2,000,000 shares/day
	•	Has a daily volatility of 2%
	•	You buy 20,000 shares (1%)

Then:
\text{Impact} \approx 0.5 \times 2\% \times \sqrt{\frac{20,000}{2,000,000}} = 0.5 \times 2\% \times 0.1 = 0.1\%

So your trade might move the price by about 0.1%, depending on how you execute it.

⸻

⚙️ How Traders Reduce Impact

Institutions use:
	•	VWAP or TWAP algorithms (trade gradually across time)
	•	Dark pools (to avoid revealing intent)
	•	Liquidity-sensitive execution (pause when volume is low)

⸻

import math

def market_impact(Q, V, sigma, c=0.5):
    """
    Estimate the percentage market impact of a trade using the square-root model.
    
    Parameters:
        Q (float): Trade size in shares
        V (float): Average daily volume in shares
        sigma (float): Daily volatility (e.g., 0.02 for 2%)
        c (float): Market impact constant (default 0.5)
    
    Returns:
        float: Estimated market impact as a percentage
    """
    impact = c * sigma * math.sqrt(Q / V)
    return impact * 100  # Convert to percentage

no. im curious about this because a lot of people say when paper trading they make money with a strategy but once they start trading real money it doesn’t work. is this because their order for paper trading aren’t being priced into the market or is it because people start to deviate from strategy when it’s real money vs paper? 

Excellent question — and you’re thinking about this exactly the right way.
The short answer is: it’s both, but in very different ways depending on the type of strategy and scale of trading. Let’s unpack this carefully.

⸻

🧠 1. The Psychological Factor (the Human difference)

For most retail traders, the biggest reason performance drops from paper trading to live trading is behavioral:
	•	Risk feels real: In paper trading, losses are just numbers. In live trading, a $500 drawdown feels like pain — it triggers fear, second-guessing, and hesitation.
	•	Greed and impatience: When it’s real money, people take profits too early or refuse to cut losers, breaking the edge of the strategy.
	•	Inconsistency: They skip trades that “look bad” or overtrade because they want to “make back” a loss.
	•	Execution mistakes: Real trading involves slippage, order type selection, and fills that paper accounts often simplify.

In short, discipline breaks down when emotions enter the equation.
That alone explains the majority of why most paper-proven systems fail live for small traders.

⸻

⚙️ 2. The Structural Factor (the Market Mechanics difference)

Now, for larger traders — or anyone whose trades are big enough to move price even slightly — the mechanics of real orders matter.
Paper trading does not simulate this properly.

Here’s why:
Paper Trading	Real Trading
Fills are assumed instantly at the displayed price	You get filled only if there’s liquidity at that price
No slippage	Slippage happens when your order consumes liquidity and moves the price
No spread cost or hidden fees	You pay the bid–ask spread, exchange fees, and possibly routing costs
Market impact is ignored	Large orders can move the market against you
So if your backtested or paper strategy relies on entering/exiting exactly at the shown prices — especially with short-term or scalping strategies — those results are inflated.

Even a 0.05% difference in entry/exit due to slippage or spread can destroy the edge of a high-frequency or intraday system.

⸻

🧩 3. The Invisible Difference: Market Feedback

There’s another subtle but critical effect:
When you trade real money, your orders become part of the market data that other algorithms react to.

If your size or timing is predictable (say, you always buy breakouts), market makers and HFT algorithms might:
	•	Fade your orders (sell into your buy),
	•	Widen spreads temporarily, or
	•	Front-run you with faster routing.

Paper trading never includes this reflexivity — your trades don’t influence the order book or cause liquidity providers to adapt.

So, your strategy is being “priced in” to the real market once you go live.

⸻

🧮 4. How Much of Each Factor Matters
Factor	Who It Affects Most	Typical Impact
Emotions / Psychology	Retail traders	Large (strategy deviation)
Slippage & Spread	Day traders / scalpers	Moderate to large
Market Impact	Large or institutional traders	Large
Execution latency / fills	Algorithmic & HFT	Very large
Liquidity illusion (paper vs. real)	Everyone trading thin stocks or low-volume assets	Moderate

🧭 5. How to Make Paper Trading More “Real”

To truly test a strategy, you can:
	•	Add friction: include estimated slippage (e.g., 0.02–0.05%) and spread costs.
	•	Simulate commissions.
	•	Test with limit orders (which may not always fill).
	•	Record psychological reactions (note how you would’ve felt).
	•	Start small live: Trade with tiny position sizes to observe emotional and execution differences.

⸻

TL;DR Summary
Cause	Why it happens
Psychological	Fear, greed, and inconsistency make traders deviate from their plan when money is real.
Mechanical	Real orders interact with spreads, slippage, and market depth — paper trades don’t.
Reflexive	Your trades actually affect the market once they’re real — even subtly.
