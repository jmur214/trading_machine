# The Trading Machine Team (Plain English Engine Roles)

If our trading system was a room full of human professionals working at a prestigious hedge fund, here is exactly who would be in the room, what their job title is, and what single question they are responsible for answering.

The golden rule of the room: **No one is allowed to do someone else's job.**

---

## 1. The Researcher (Engine A)
*Also known as: The Forecast Engine*

**The core question they answer:** *"What is a good opportunity right now, and how strongly do I believe in it?"*

The Researcher spends all day looking at price charts, company fundamentals, news, and mathematical patterns (our "edges"). When they see a setup they like, they slide a piece of paper across the table that says: "I think we should bet on Apple going UP, and I am 85% confident in this."

**What they do NOT do:** They don't decide how much money to bet. They don't check if we have enough cash. They don't care if the market is crashing—if they see a mathematical reason Apple will go up, they report it. They are purely the idea generator.

---

## 2. The Macro Thinker (Engine E)
*Also known as: The Regime Intelligence Engine*

**The core question they answer:** *"What kind of market environment are we currently playing in?"*

The Macro Thinker stands at the window looking at the weather. They don't look at individual stocks; they look at the whole stock market, volatility (fear), and the economy. Every day, they announce to the room: "It is currently a high-volatility bear market, and I am 90% sure it will stay this way for a while."

**What they do NOT do:** They never pick stocks. They never place trades. They just provide the weather report so the rest of the team knows whether to wear a raincoat or sunglasses.

---

## 3. The Risk Manager (Engine B)
*Also known as: The Trade Construction & Execution Engine*

**The core question they answer:** *"How do we survive if the Researcher is wrong?"*

The Risk Manager takes the piece of paper from the Researcher (the trade idea) and looks at the weather report from the Macro Thinker. Their only goal is to make sure one bad trade doesn't bankrupt the fund. They say: "Okay, I approve the Apple trade. But because the weather is highly volatile right now, we are only going to buy 100 shares instead of 500, and we are setting a strict stop-loss at -$50."

**What they do NOT do:** They never invent trade ideas. They never try to predict if Apple is going up or down. Their only job is to put a seatbelt on the trade before letting it out the door.

---

## 4. The Portfolio Manager (Engine C - Allocation)
*Also known as: The Capital Allocator*

**The core question they answer:** *"Where should our total pool of money be distributed?"*

The Portfolio Manager looks at all the approved ideas on the table and asks how they fit together. They might say: "We love Apple and Microsoft, but we already have too much money in technology stocks. For safety through diversification, we need to put some of our money into healthcare." They decide the ideal pie chart of where the fund's money *should* be.

**What they do NOT do:** They don't micromanage individual trade entries or exits. They care about the forest, not the trees.

---

## 5. The Accountant (Engine C - Ledger)
*Also known as: The Bookkeeper*

**The core question they answer:** *"What exactly do we own right now, and how much money do we have?"*

The Accountant is the most rigid person in the room. They don't care about predictions, weather, or risk. They just record the unforgiving truth. They log every single dollar spent, every share bought, and every fee paid. If anyone in the room wants to know exactly how much cash is left in the bank, they ask the Accountant.

**What they do NOT do:** They never guess, predict, or allocate. They only record facts.

---

## 6. The Performance Reviewer (Engine D)
*Also known as: The Strategy Governor*

**The core question they answer:** *"Who is making us money, and who made a mistake?"*

The Performance Reviewer sits in the corner taking notes on everyone's performance over the last 6 months. They look at the Researcher's ideas and say: "Your 'mean reversion' ideas have been losing money for 3 months straight. Your 'momentum' ideas have been highly profitable. Starting tomorrow, we are going to trust your momentum ideas more, and trust your mean reversion ideas less."

**What they do NOT do:** They don't try to fix the trades while they are happening. They act like a coach reviewing game tape after the game is over, deciding who gets to start in tomorrow's game.

---

## Summary of the Room Workflow
1. The **Macro Thinker (E)** announces the market weather.
2. The **Researcher (A)** uses that context to find opportunities and shouts out their best ideas.
3. The **Portfolio Manager (C)** decides how those ideas should fit into an ideal pie chart.
4. The **Risk Manager (B)** takes those ideas, puts seatbelts on them, sizes them safely, and executes them.
5. The **Accountant (C)** logs the receipts to prove exactly what we bought.
6. The **Performance Reviewer (D)** watches all this happen and grades the Researcher's strategies over time, adjusting who we listen to next month.
